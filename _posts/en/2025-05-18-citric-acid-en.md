Citric acid is a naturally occurring organic acid widely recognized for its versatility in food, industrial, pharmaceutical, and household applications. Chemically classified as a tricarboxylic acid, it plays a significant role in biological and industrial processes due to its unique properties, such as acidity, chelating ability, and solubility. This introduction provides a detailed overview of citric acid, covering its chemical structure, natural occurrence, production methods, applications, safety, and environmental impact.

---

#### 1. **Chemical and Physical Properties**
- **Chemical Formula**: C₆H₈O₇
- **Molecular Weight**: 192.12 g/mol
- **Structure**: Citric acid is a tricarboxylic acid with three carboxyl groups (-COOH) and one hydroxyl group (-OH). Its chemical name is 2-hydroxypropane-1,2,3-tricarboxylic acid.
- **Appearance**: In its anhydrous form, citric acid appears as colorless or white crystalline granules or powder. It is also available in a monohydrate form with one water molecule.
- **Solubility**: Highly soluble in water (approximately 147 g/100 mL at 20°C) and soluble in ethanol, but insoluble in non-polar solvents like benzene or chloroform.
- **pH**: Citric acid is a weak organic acid with a pH of about 3.2 in a 1% aqueous solution. It dissociates in water, releasing hydrogen ions in three steps (pKa values: 3.13, 4.76, 6.40).
- **Taste**: Sour, tart flavor, which makes it a popular acidulant in food and beverages.

#### 2. **Natural Occurrence**
Citric acid is ubiquitous in nature, found in many fruits and vegetables, particularly citrus fruits, from which it derives its name (from the Latin *citrus*). It is a key intermediate in the **Krebs cycle** (or citric acid cycle), a fundamental metabolic pathway in aerobic organisms that generates energy through the oxidation of acetyl-CoA.
- **Sources**:
  - **Citrus fruits**: Lemons (5–6% citric acid), limes (5–6%), oranges (0.8–1%), and grapefruits.
  - Other fruits: Berries (e.g., raspberries, blackberries), pineapples, and tomatoes.
  - Vegetables: Broccoli and potatoes (in smaller amounts).
- **Biological Role**: In living organisms, citric acid is produced during cellular respiration in mitochondria, facilitating energy production by acting as an intermediate in the Krebs cycle.

#### 3. **Historical Context**
- Citric acid was first isolated in 1784 by Swedish chemist **Carl Wilhelm Scheele** from lemon juice.
- Until the early 20th century, citric acid was primarily extracted from citrus fruits, mainly in Italy, which dominated the market.
- In 1917, American scientist **James Currie** discovered that the mold *Aspergillus niger* could produce citric acid from sugar substrates, leading to the development of industrial microbial fermentation. This revolutionized production, making citric acid more affordable and widely available.

#### 4. **Production Methods**
Today, over 99% of citric acid is produced via microbial fermentation, with only a small fraction derived from natural sources due to cost and scalability.

- **Microbial Fermentation**:
  - **Microorganism**: *Aspergillus niger* is the primary mold used, though other strains like *Candida* species are occasionally employed.
  - **Substrates**: Cheap carbon sources like molasses, corn syrup, or glucose are used as feedstocks.
  - **Process**:
    1. The mold is cultured in a bioreactor with a sugar substrate under controlled conditions (pH, temperature, aeration).
    2. Citric acid is secreted into the medium as a metabolic byproduct.
    3. The broth is filtered, purified, and crystallized to produce anhydrous or monohydrate citric acid.
  - **Yield**: Modern processes achieve high yields, producing over 2 million metric tons annually worldwide, with China as the leading producer.

- **Natural Extraction**:
  - Rarely used commercially due to high costs.
  - Involves pressing citrus fruits, concentrating the juice, and crystallizing the acid.

- **Synthetic Production**:
  - Chemical synthesis is possible but not economically viable compared to fermentation.

#### 5. **Applications**
Citric acid’s versatility stems from its acidity, chelating properties (binding metal ions), and safety for human consumption. Its applications span multiple industries:

- **Food and Beverage Industry (60–70% of global use)**:
  - **Acidulant**: Adjusts pH and enhances tartness in soft drinks (e.g., colas, lemonades), candies, jams, and jellies.
  - **Preservative**: Inhibits microbial growth and extends shelf life in canned foods, sauces, and beverages.
  - **Flavor Enhancer**: Balances sweetness and adds a tangy note.
  - **Antioxidant Synergist**: Prevents oxidation in fats and oils when combined with antioxidants like ascorbic acid.
  - **Examples**: Found in sodas, fruit-flavored snacks, ice creams, and processed cheeses.

- **Pharmaceuticals and Cosmetics (10–15% of use)**:
  - **Pharmaceuticals**: Used as an excipient in effervescent tablets (e.g., Alka-Seltzer), where it reacts with sodium bicarbonate to produce carbon dioxide. Also stabilizes active ingredients and adjusts pH in syrups and creams.
  - **Cosmetics**: Acts as a pH adjuster, exfoliant (in alpha-hydroxy acid formulations), and chelating agent in shampoos, creams, and face masks. It brightens skin and reduces signs of aging.
  - **Medical Uses**: Used in anticoagulant solutions (e.g., sodium citrate) to prevent blood clotting during transfusions.

- **Industrial Applications (15–20% of use)**:
  - **Detergents and Cleaners**: Replaces phosphates in eco-friendly detergents due to its ability to soften water by chelating calcium and magnesium ions. Found in dishwashing liquids, laundry detergents, and descaling agents.
  - **Metal Finishing**: Removes rust and scale from metal surfaces in industrial cleaning.
  - **Plastics and Textiles**: Used in the production of biodegradable plastics and as a mordant in dyeing fabrics.

- **Other Uses**:
  - **Agriculture**: Adjusts soil pH and enhances nutrient availability.
  - **Photography**: Used in some developing solutions.
  - **Environmental Applications**: Aids in bioremediation by chelating heavy metals in contaminated soils.

#### 6. **Safety and Regulatory Status**
- **Safety**: Citric acid is classified as **Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS)** by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and is approved for use in food by the European Union (E-number E330), Codex Alimentarius, and other regulatory bodies.
- **Consumption**: Safe in moderate amounts, as it is naturally metabolized by the body. Excessive intake (e.g., from highly acidic foods) may cause dental enamel erosion or mild gastrointestinal discomfort.
- **Allergies**: Rare, but some individuals may experience sensitivity, often due to impurities in low-quality citric acid derived from mold fermentation (e.g., corn-based substrates).
- **Occupational Exposure**: Prolonged exposure to citric acid dust may cause respiratory or skin irritation in industrial settings, requiring proper handling.

#### 7. **Environmental Impact**

- **Biodegradability**: Citric acid is fully biodegradable and does not persist in the environment, making it a preferred alternative to phosphates in detergents.
- **Sustainability**: Efforts are underway to optimize fermentation processes, reduce water usage, and utilize renewable feedstocks to enhance sustainability.

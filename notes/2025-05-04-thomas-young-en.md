Thomas Young (1773–1829) was an English polymath whose extraordinary contributions spanned physics, medicine, linguistics, and Egyptology, earning him the moniker "The Last Man Who Knew Everything." Born on June 13, 1773, in Milverton, Somerset, into a Quaker family, Young displayed prodigious intellect from an early age, reading fluently by age two and mastering multiple languages, including Latin, Greek, French, and Italian, by his teens. His insatiable curiosity and rigorous self-education laid the foundation for a career that profoundly shaped modern science and humanities.

### Early Life and Education
Young’s intellectual gifts were nurtured by his family and tutors. He studied medicine at the Universities of London, Edinburgh, and Göttingen, earning his medical degree in 1796. His medical training was complemented by a deep interest in natural philosophy (the precursor to modern science), particularly optics and mechanics. At Cambridge University, where he studied from 1797, he was nicknamed "Phenomenon Young" for his encyclopedic knowledge. His early work in medicine included significant contributions to understanding the human eye, notably his 1793 paper on the mechanism of accommodation (how the eye focuses), which proposed that the lens changes shape—a theory later confirmed.

### Contributions to Physics
Young is best known for his groundbreaking work in optics and wave theory. In the early 1800s, he challenged Isaac Newton’s corpuscular (particle) theory of light, advocating instead for the wave theory. His most famous experiment, the **double-slit experiment** (1801), demonstrated light’s interference patterns, providing compelling evidence that light behaves as a wave. This work laid the groundwork for modern quantum mechanics and optics. He introduced the concept of **interference** and coined terms like "wavelength" and "coherence," which remain fundamental to physics.

Young also made seminal contributions to mechanics. He introduced the **Young’s modulus**, a measure of a material’s stiffness, which is critical in engineering and materials science. His work on elasticity and surface tension further showcased his ability to bridge theoretical and applied science.

### Medical and Physiological Contributions
As a physician at St. George’s Hospital in London, Young advanced medical science. His studies on the eye extended to color vision, where he proposed (independently of Hermann von Helmholtz) the **trichromatic theory**, suggesting that the retina contains three types of color receptors for red, green, and blue. This theory became a cornerstone of vision science. He also contributed to understanding blood circulation and respiration, applying physical principles to biological systems.

### Linguistics and Egyptology
Young’s intellectual versatility shone in his work on the Rosetta Stone, a key to deciphering ancient Egyptian hieroglyphs. Between 1814 and 1819, he made significant progress in identifying phonetic and ideographic elements of the script, laying critical groundwork for Jean-François Champollion’s later decipherment. Young’s linguistic prowess extended to compiling a comparative vocabulary of 400 languages and dialects, showcasing his ability to synthesize vast datasets.

### Other Endeavors
Young’s curiosity knew no bounds. He served as secretary of the Royal Society, contributed to naval engineering (advising on ship design), and even wrote on music theory, analyzing the physics of sound. His work on tides and pendulums further demonstrated his knack for applying mathematics to natural phenomena. He also authored influential articles for the *Encyclopædia Britannica*, disseminating knowledge across disciplines.

### Personal Life and Legacy
Despite his brilliance, Young was known for modesty and a reserved demeanor, which sometimes limited his public recognition during his lifetime. His dense, mathematical writing style made his ideas less accessible, and he often faced resistance, particularly from Newton’s defenders in the optics debates. He married Eliza Maxwell in 1804, but they had no children. Young’s health declined in his later years, and he died on May 10, 1829, in London.

Young’s legacy is immense. His wave theory of light paved the way for 19th-century physics, influencing figures like Augustin-Jean Fresnel and James Clerk Maxwell. Young’s modulus remains a staple in engineering, and his trichromatic theory underpins modern color science. In Egyptology, his Rosetta Stone work was pivotal. Though less celebrated than contemporaries like Humphry Davy, Young’s interdisciplinary genius has earned him posthumous acclaim as a titan of the Enlightenment.

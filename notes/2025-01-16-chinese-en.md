### I. Overview of Exam Content
1. **Overview of Classical Chinese Literature**: Understanding the origins, schools, development, and influence of classical Chinese literature on later periods.
2. **Historical Phases of Classical Chinese Literature**: Major literary stages from the Pre-Qin period to the Qing dynasty and their representative works.
3. **Ideological Background of Classical Literature**: The influence of Confucianism, Daoism, and Legalism on literature, as well as the role of historical context in shaping literary schools.
4. **The Relationship Between Society and Literature**: How social changes, political turmoil, and cultural traditions influenced literary creation.
5. **Artistic Characteristics of Classical Chinese Literature**: The rhetorical techniques, expressive forms, poetry structure, symbolism, and emotional expression in classical literature.
6. **Classical Chinese Literature in a Global Context**: Analyzing the place and influence of classical Chinese literature in world literary traditions.

### II. Classical Chinese Literature and Representative Works
7. **The Book of Songs (Shi Jing)**: Analyzing its historical significance as China’s earliest anthology of poetry, divided into three sections: Feng, Ya, and Song.
8. **Language and Artistic Features of the Book of Songs**: The use of simple, plain language, depiction of daily life and nature, and the employment of parallelism and antithesis.
9. **Emotional Expression in the Book of Songs**: The portrayal of themes such as love, politics, and social issues.
10. **Chu Ci (Songs of Chu)**: The background and literary contributions of Qu Yuan and other poets, focusing on works like *Li Sao* (The Lament).
11. **The Literary Status of Qu Yuan**: Qu Yuan’s impact on Chinese classical literature, particularly the symbolic and emotional expression in *Li Sao*.
12. **Analysis of *Li Sao***: A deep dive into the structure, rhetoric, and emotional content of *Li Sao*.
13. **Han Dynasty Literature**: The development of literature in the Han dynasty, particularly historical texts such as *Shi Ji* (Records of the Grand Historian) and *Han Shu* (Book of Han).
14. **Literary Features of *Shi Ji* and *Han Shu***: The literary and historical value of these historical texts, including character development and narrative style.
15. **Tang Dynasty Literature**: The flourishing of Tang poetry, prose, and other forms, with particular attention to the artistic evolution in poetry.
16. **Artistic Features of Tang Poetry**: The structure, themes, and stylistic innovations of Tang poetry, highlighting rhythm, rhyme, and balance.
17. **The Poetry of Li Bai**: Exploring the bold, free-spirited style of Li Bai, characterized by naturalism and romanticism.
18. **The Poetry of Du Fu**: Analyzing Du Fu’s melancholic, deep, and socially critical poetry.
19. **The Landscape Poetry of Wang Wei**: Wang Wei’s integration of nature and Zen philosophy in his poetry.
20. **Poetic Styles of Tang Poets**: Comparative analysis of the individual styles of Tang poets like Li Bai, Du Fu, Bai Juyi, and Wang Wei.
21. **The Art of Song Dynasty Ci Poetry**: The evolution of *ci* poetry, focusing on its rhythm and musicality, with an emphasis on poets like Su Shi and Xin Qiji.
22. **Analysis of Su Shi’s Ci Poetry**: Su Shi’s blend of boldness and philosophical depth in poems like *Shui Diao Ge Tou*.
23. **Analysis of Xin Qiji’s Ci Poetry**: Xin Qiji’s passionate and nationalistic approach to *ci* poetry, exemplified by works like *Qingyuanshi*.
24. **Yuan Dynasty Drama**: The rise of Yuan drama, including the development of *zaju* (a form of Chinese opera) and *qu* (songs).
25. **Major Yuan Playwrights**: Key figures such as Guan Hanqing and Zheng Guangzu, and their contributions to Chinese theater.

### III. Classical Literary Theory
26. **Literary Thoughts in the Pre-Qin Period**: The influence of Confucianism, Daoism, and Legalism on early Chinese literary creation.
27. **Confucian Literary Thought**: Confucian values like virtue, loyalty, and propriety in literary works, with a focus on social responsibility and moral guidance.
28. **Daoist Literary Thought**: The influence of Daoism’s natural philosophy on literary works, advocating for spontaneity and simplicity.
29. **Legalist Literary Thought**: Emphasizing order and authority, with works focusing on practical governance and societal structure.
30. **Characteristics of Pre-Qin Prose**: The straightforward, philosophical, and reflective nature of early prose, as seen in texts like *Lunyu* (Analects) and *Zhuangzi*.
31. **Literary Value of *Zuozhuan* and *Guoyu***: Examining the literary techniques in these historical texts, particularly in character portrayal and narrative structure.
32. **Rhetorical Techniques in Classical Poetry**: The use of *bi-xing* (metaphor and allusion), parallelism, and antithesis in shaping classical poetry.
33. **Literary Style in *Lunyu* (Analects)**: The simplicity, conciseness, and moral insights that characterize Confucian literature.
34. **Rhetoric in *Zhuangzi* (Daoist Text)**: The use of allegory, parable, and paradox in Daoist thought as conveyed in *Zhuangzi*.
35. **Methods of Expression in Classical Poetry and Prose**: How classical Chinese writers employed sparse language to convey profound ideas and emotions.
36. **Fables and Symbolism in Classical Literature**: The use of fables and symbolic imagery in traditional works, conveying deeper meanings.
37. **Rhetorical Features of Classical Prose**: Techniques like parallelism and contrast that are used to enhance the aesthetic and intellectual appeal of classical prose.
38. **Literary Criticism in Ancient China**: The development of literary criticism in classical Chinese thought and key critical texts like *Shijing* and *Yuefu*.
39. **Social and Ideological Content in Classical Literature**: The multiple layers of meaning embedded in classical works, reflecting social, historical, and personal ideologies.
40. **Aesthetic Standards in Classical Literature**: Classical Chinese aesthetics focused on beauty, virtue, and moral harmony.

### IV. Modern Chinese Literature Overview
41. **Modern Chinese Literature Post-May Fourth Movement**: The May Fourth Movement’s influence on modern Chinese literature, including the rise of vernacular literature.
42. **Literary Movements in Modern China**: The New Culture Movement, Leftist Literature, and the rise of vernacular Chinese in literary creation.
43. **The Role of Lu Xun in Modern Chinese Literature**: Lu Xun’s status as the founding figure of modern Chinese literature, particularly through works like *A Madman's Diary* and *The True Story of Ah Q*.
44. **Social Critique in Modern Literature**: The themes of social critique and personal struggle found in works by writers like Ba Jin and Lao She.
45. **Artistic Forms in Modern Literature**: The diversification of genres in modern literature, including novels, short stories, and essays.
46. **The Relationship Between Literature and Social Change**: How literature reflects social and political changes, particularly during periods of revolution and reform.
47. **The Rise of Female Writers in Modern Chinese Literature**: The emergence of female writers like Ding Ling and Xiao Hong, and their exploration of gender and social issues.
48. **Modern Poetry and Language Innovation**: The linguistic and formal innovations in modern Chinese poetry, particularly the shift to vernacular language.
49. **Modern Novel Narrative Techniques**: Innovations in structure, character development, and plot design in the modern Chinese novel.
50. **Modern Essays and Autobiographical Literature**: The development of essays and autobiographical writing in the modern period, with writers like Lu Xun, Guo Moruo, and Liang Shiqiu.

### V. Classical and Modern Literary Works
51. **Analysis of *Nahan* (Call to Arms)**: Lu Xun’s influence on modern Chinese literature, especially his short story *Diary of a Madman*.
52. **Analysis of *Wandering* (彷徨)**: The exploration of the inner conflicts and dilemmas of modern individuals, as seen in works by Lu Xun.
53. **Analysis of *Midnight* (子夜)**: Ba Jin’s depiction of the social and political turbulence in modern China.
54. **Analysis of *Family* (家)**: Ba Jin’s *Family* as an exploration of the traditional family system’s decline.
55. **Analysis of *White Deer Plain* (白鹿原)**: Chen Zhongshi’s portrayal of rural China’s transformation over several generations.
56. **Analysis of *Fortress Besieged* (围城)**: Qian Zhongshu’s satirical examination of intellectuals and modern Chinese society.
57. **Development of Modern Poetry**: The progression of poetry from the May Fourth movement’s free verse to contemporary Chinese poetry.
58. **New Moon Poetry and Symbolism**: The influence of Western Symbolism on the New Moon Poetry group, including poets like Xu Zhimo and Li Jinfa.
59. **Modern Prose Style**: The development of modern prose in China, particularly from writers like Lu Xun and Liang Qichao.
60. **Modern Drama and Theatre**: The emergence of modern

 Chinese drama, influenced by Western theatre traditions.
61. **Literature in the Cultural Revolution**: The impact of political movements like the Cultural Revolution on literature, particularly through the promotion of revolutionary literature.

### VI. Foreign Literature and Its Influence
62. **Shakespeare’s Works**: Analyzing Shakespeare’s plays and poems, and their profound influence on world literature.
63. **The Golden Age of Russian Literature**: Contributions of Russian authors like Pushkin, Tolstoy, and Dostoevsky to global literature.
64. **French Romantic Literature**: The works of Hugo, Balzac, Flaubert, and others in the context of French Romanticism.
65. **German Literary Philosophy**: The literary contributions of German writers like Goethe and Nietzsche, particularly their philosophical exploration of the human condition.
66. **The Development of American Literature**: The formation of American literature and contributions by authors like Faulkner and Hemingway.
67. **Modernist Literature Features**: The rise of literary modernism, particularly through techniques like stream of consciousness and symbolism, as seen in Joyce’s *Ulysses*.
68. **Translation and Spread of Foreign Literature**: How foreign literature entered China through translation and its influence on Chinese writers and literary practices.
69. **Foreign Literature's Influence on Chinese Modern Literature**: The role of Western literary theories and techniques in shaping Chinese modernist literature.

76. **Literary Criticism**: Theoretical foundations of literary criticism, including methods to analyze and evaluate literary works.
77. **Poetry Creation Techniques**: Understanding the language, form, and emotional expression in modern poetry.
78. **Fiction Writing Techniques**: The use of plot, character, and conflict in creating compelling novels.
79. **Essay Writing Techniques**: The art of self-expression and social critique in essay writing.
80. **Drama Creation Techniques**: Analyzing how dialogue, stage setting, and character conflict are used in drama to convey themes.
